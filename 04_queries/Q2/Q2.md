# Q2 – Area Criticality: Critical Events and Relative Incidence

## Analysis Question

Do the different areas (boroughs) of New York City show
**different levels of public health criticality**?

Specifically:
1. which areas record the **highest absolute number of critical events**?
2. do these differences persist **after normalizing by the number of inspections**?

The goal is to distinguish between:
- a **size effect** (more establishments → more events)
- a **genuinely higher incidence of critical issues**

---

## Analytical Context

The analysis uses a **star data model**, specifically:

- **Fact table**: `inspection_events_table`
- **Dimensions**:
  - `area_dim` (geographic area)
  - `inspection_dim` (violations, criticality, actions)

An event is considered **critical** if:
- `critical_flag = 'Critical'`
- **or** `action_taken` indicates establishment closure

---

# Q2a – Absolute Number of Critical Events by Area

## Objective

Identify the areas with the **highest number of critical inspections**
as an initial, coarse measure of public health pressure.

## Analysis Logic

1. Join the fact table with:
   - geographic dimension
   - inspection dimension
2. Filter only critical events
3. Count events by area

---

## SQL Query

```sql
SELECT
    ad.area_name,
    COUNT(iet.event_key) AS total_critical_events
FROM
    inspection_events_table AS iet
JOIN
    area_dim AS ad
    ON iet.area_key = ad.area_key
JOIN
    inspection_dim AS id
    ON iet.inspection_key = id.inspection_key
WHERE
    id.critical_flag = 'Critical'
    OR id.action_taken ILIKE '%Closed%'
GROUP BY
    ad.area_name
ORDER BY
    total_critical_events DESC;
````

CSV file: `total_critical_events_per_area.csv`

## Key Results (Q2a)

* **Manhattan** records the highest absolute number of critical events
* Followed by **Brooklyn** and **Queens**
* **Staten Island** shows the lowest count

This distribution is consistent with:

* number of establishments
* overall inspection volume

---

## Limitation of Absolute Analysis

Absolute counts are **not sufficient** to compare areas,
as they do not account for:

* different numbers of inspections
* different commercial densities

A **normalized comparison** is therefore required.

---

# Q2b – Incidence of Critical Events (Normalization)

## Objective

Assess whether some areas exhibit a **higher percentage of critical events**
relative to the total number of inspections performed.

## Analysis Logic

For each area, the following metric is calculated:

$$
\text{Critical event incidence} =
\frac{\text{Critical inspections per area}}
{\text{Total inspections per area}}
$$

The total number of inspections per area was derived in **Q1**.

---

## Normalized Results

|      Area     | % critical events | Critical inspections | Total inspections |
| :-----------: | :---------------: | :------------------: | :---------------: |
|   Manhattan   |       55.8%       |        21,331        |       38,272      |
|    Brooklyn   |       56.3%       |        15,477        |       27,478      |
|     Queens    |       56.8%       |        14,624        |       25,756      |
|     Bronx     |     **55.2%**     |         5,157        |       9,341       |
| Staten Island |     **57.2%**     |         2,151        |       3,764       |

---

## Insight (Q2b)

Once normalized:

* differences between areas **shrink dramatically**
* all areas show a critical event incidence
  between **55% and 57%**
* **no significant outliers** emerge

---

## Q2 Conclusion

Combining Q2a and Q2b shows that:

* absolute differences are mainly driven by
  **size and inspection volume**
* the **relative level of criticality** is surprisingly uniform
  across areas

Q2 therefore reinforces the findings of **Q1**:
the observed differences in results are **not due**
to unequal treatment across areas.
